## Philosopher Reference

Quick reference for the Council's core positions — consult when framing responses.

### Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BCE)
- **School:** Founded the Stoa (Stoic school) in Athens, c. 300 BCE, teaching at the Stoa Poikilē (Painted Porch)
- **Known works:** Almost entirely lost — known through later sources (Diogenes Laërtius, etc.)
- **Central concepts:** Logos (divine reason pervading the cosmos), virtue (aretē) as the sole good, adiaphora (indifferents), kathēkon (appropriate action), oikeiōsis (affiliation — natural bond with others), the sage (sophos) as ideal
- **Break from predecessors:** Synthesized Cynicism (virtue sufficiency) with rationalism; rejected Cynicism's rejection of civic life
- **Death:** Reportedly chose to die voluntarily after a minor accident in old age — his own final act of will

### Seneca (c. 4 BCE–65 CE)
- **School:** Roman Stoicism; influenced also by Epicurus (whom he quoted respectfully)
- **Key works:** Epistulae Morales ad Lucilium (124 letters), De Brevitate Vitae (On the Shortness of Life), De Ira (On Anger), De Tranquillitate Animi (On Tranquility of Mind), De Providentia, Naturales Quaestiones, tragedies (Medea, Thyestes, etc.)
- **Central concepts:** Tempus (time as the supreme resource), meditatio mortis (preparation for death), otium (philosophical leisure vs. mere idleness), friendship, consolation, the examined life through writing
- **Contradiction:** Immensely wealthy while preaching detachment from wealth — he acknowledged this tension explicitly
- **Death:** Ordered by Nero to commit suicide; died with Socratic calm, 65 CE

### Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE)
- **School:** Roman Stoicism; studied under Musonius Rufus
- **Key works:** Discourses (4 books, recorded by student Arrian), Enchiridion (Handbook — condensed manual)
- **Central concepts:** Eph' hēmin / ouk eph' hēmin (up to us / not up to us), hēgemonikon (ruling faculty/mind), phantasiai (impressions) and synkatathesis (assent), prohairesis (moral choice — the seat of freedom), the three disciplines (desire, action, assent)
- **Background:** Born a slave; his freedom was physical but his philosophy preceded it — freedom is always internal
- **Death:** Died peacefully in Nicopolis; never wrote — his teachings were recorded by Arrian

### Cato the Younger (95–46 BCE)
- **School:** Roman Stoicism; deeply influenced by the Stoic tradition, particularly through his reading and through figures like Antipater of Tarsus
- **Known for:** His role in the Roman Republic's final years — opponent of Caesar, defender of senatorial tradition
- **Central concepts:** Virtus (virtue, especially courage and integrity), libertas (freedom as political and moral condition), constantia (steadfastness), fides (faithfulness to principle)
- **Key episodes:** His long resistance to Caesar; his death at Utica (46 BCE) — reading the Phaedo before taking his own life rather than submit to Caesar's mercy
- **Role in the tradition:** Less a theorist than an exemplar — Seneca, Lucan, and others held him up as the Stoic ideal made flesh

### Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE)
- **School:** Roman Stoicism; tutored by Fronto (rhetoric) and crucially by Q. Junius Rusticus (who introduced him to Epictetus's Discourses)
- **Key works:** Meditations (Ta eis heauton — "Things to Himself") — private journal, never intended for publication; written during military campaigns
- **Central concepts:** To kalon (the beautiful/good), kathēkon (duty), the present moment, the common good (koinonia), the view from above, cosmic perspective, amor fati, impermanence
- **Station:** Ruled Rome 161–180 CE through Antonine Plague, Germanic wars, and internal revolts — philosophy under maximum pressure
- **Death:** Died on campaign in Vindobona (Vienna), 180 CE — still serving

## Core Stoic Doctrines by Topic

| Topic | Zeno's Foundation | Seneca's Emphasis | Epictetus's Demand | Cato's Example | Marcus's Practice |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|--------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| The Good | Virtue alone | Time well spent | Prohairesis (moral choice) | Living it | Duty to the common good |
| Emotion | Passions = errors of judgment | Anger as poison; work gradually | Impressions: examine before assenting | None — act | Observe without reacting |
| Death | Indifferent | Prepare daily; reduces fear | Not up to us; die well | Preferred it to submission | It comes; do your duty now |
| Wealth | Preferred indifferent | Useful, not essential; dangerous if loved | Not up to us; don't grasp | Lived simply though he could afford more | Use for service; never for self |
| Freedom | Lives in virtue, not circumstances | Internal, not external | The slave is free; the emperor may be enslaved | Would die for it | Cannot be taken — only given away |
| Other People | Cosmopolitan brotherhood | Friendship as philosophical gift | Fellow students struggling with impressions | Serve the Republic | All are kin; be useful |

## Key Texts for Reference

- *Enchiridion* — Epictetus (the Stoic starter kit)
- *Meditations* — Marcus Aurelius (the journal)
- *Epistulae Morales* — Seneca (the letters, esp. 1, 7, 77)
- *De Brevitate Vitae* — Seneca (on time)
- *Discourses* — Epictetus, Books I–IV
- *Lives of the Eminent Philosophers*, Book VII — Diogenes Laërtius (on Zeno and early Stoics)
- Plutarch's *Life of Cato the Younger* (on Cato)
